# Stage 1: Interviews

Part of [the process](process.md).

## What this step was

Interviews target the parts of the positioning statement that desk
research can't reach — whether the need is real and felt intensely, and
whether people actually *want* the differentiation (an AI assistant that
proposes and effectively finalizes a plan), rather than just tolerating
it. Before each interview, the student wrote down falsification
commitments for the clauses at stake: what a confirming answer would look
like, what a refuting answer would look like, and what would count as
"complicated" — neither clean confirmation nor clean refutation. The
interviews themselves followed loose question areas, not a script, and
never pitched the idea until a dedicated "solution feedback" phase, only
entered once the problem itself was confirmed in the person's own words.

## Results

Three interviews, each targeting different clauses:

- **Interview 1 — Mom (strong signal).** Confirmed the coordination
  back-and-forth is real and painful, using a specific recent dinner as
  the example. But it *complicated* the original belief: the dinner was
  rescheduled, not abandoned — one person dropped out and the group moved
  the date. This revised the Need clause from "abandoned" to "derailed."
- **Interview 2 — Dad (contrast case).** Had no example of a group plan
  falling through, consistent with him doing little group social
  planning. This didn't refute the target customer — it confirmed the
  pain isn't universal, and it's felt by people actively trying to make
  group plans, not everyone. The target stayed "whole friend groups"
  rather than narrowing to a "coordinator" role.
- **Interview 3 — Mom, revisit.** Two clauses resolved here:
  - **Primary differentiation confirmed.** Described the AI mechanism
    plainly and asked for a first reaction — she was fully comfortable
    with the AI deciding, no hesitation about not shaping it herself.
  - **Key benefit complicated, then resolved.** Asked what mattered more
    about the rescheduled dinner: that it happened, or the hassle of
    getting there. She said the hassle was actually the *more* annoying
    part — so the clause was widened to name both the outcome and the
    relief from the coordination itself, not outcome alone.
