# Opportunity Notes

## Friction observed

### Story 1 — Last-minute movie plans (one-on-one)

I was trying to go to the movies with a friend, and we didn't land on a
plan until the last minute. Coordination happened entirely over text:
waiting for my friend to propose a time, then waiting for me to confirm
whether I could make it. It worked out in the end, but barely — there was
real risk we'd run out of showtimes or the plan would fall through
altogether.

This kind of last-minute scramble happens often when making plans with
friends, not just this one time.

### Story 2 — Skipped birthday parties (group)

For the last couple of birthdays, I didn't have a party at all — because
coordinating a group's schedules felt too hard to even attempt. The
friction here isn't "the party was harder to plan than I'd like," it's
severe enough that I opted out of having an event at all.

## Current coping / alternatives

- No scheduling tools or apps are used today — coordination is done
  entirely through texting.
- For one-on-one plans: friend proposes a time, I check my availability,
  back-and-forth continues until something lands, often at the last
  minute.
- For groups: the texting approach doesn't scale. The friction is high
  enough that events get abandoned before they're even planned.

## Patterns

- Friction scales badly with group size — one-on-one is annoying but
  survivable; group plans frequently don't happen at all.
- The core bottleneck seems to be that each person's availability and
  preferences live in their own head/calendar, with no shared view, so
  every plan starts from zero information.

## Candidate opportunity (early, untested)

My own framing of a possible solution: a **shared calendar where an AI
takes into account each person's existing events and the kinds of
activities they do or don't like doing**, to cut down the back-and-forth
of proposing times and activities.

This is a starting hypothesis, not yet validated — Stage 1 will turn this
into a positioning statement and identify what evidence would support or
falsify it.
