# Stage 0: Opportunity Discovery

Part of [the process](process.md).

## What this step was

Before proposing any solution, the first step was to write down real
friction — moments that actually happened, not hypothetical problems.
The rule at this stage is strict: describe what happened and how it felt,
look for a pattern across more than one incident, and only *then* let a
candidate idea surface — clearly labeled as an untested guess, not a
conclusion.

## Results

Two firsthand stories anchored the project:

- **Last-minute movie plans (one-on-one).** Coordinating over text,
  waiting on a back-and-forth of proposed times and confirmations, with
  real risk the plan would fall through. It worked out, but barely — and
  it wasn't a one-off.
- **Skipped birthdays (group).** For the last couple of years, no
  birthday party happened at all, because coordinating a whole group's
  schedules felt too hard to even attempt. This is a more severe failure
  mode than the first story: not a plan that struggled, but an event that
  never got tried.

The pattern that emerged: **friction scales badly with group size.**
One-on-one coordination is annoying but survivable; group plans
frequently don't happen at all. The underlying bottleneck is that
everyone's availability and preferences live in their own head or
calendar, with no shared view — every plan starts from zero information.

From that pattern came an early, explicitly untested idea: a shared
calendar where an AI factors in everyone's existing events and activity
preferences, to cut down the back-and-forth. This idea became the raw
material for the [positioning statement](stage-positioning.md) in Stage 1
— not a conclusion in its own right.
