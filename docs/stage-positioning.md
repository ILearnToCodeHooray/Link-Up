# Stage 1: Positioning Statement

Part of [the process](process.md).

## What this step was

A positioning statement is a single paragraph with seven required
clauses — target customer, need, product name, category, key benefit,
primary competitive alternative, and primary differentiation. The first
draft is written entirely from belief, before any outside evidence. From
there, every clause carries its own "evidence record": what's currently
believed, why, what was found through research or interviews, and —
critically — what would change the student's mind. A clause isn't
allowed to just get more confident over time; it has to survive attempts
to prove it wrong.

## Results

The current, evidence-backed statement:

> For *friend groups with busy, clashing schedules* who *want to make
> plans together but keep getting derailed in the back-and-forth — plans
> get rescheduled, downsized, or dropped, and sometimes fall through
> entirely*, **Link Up** is a *group scheduling app* that *turns "we
> should hang out" into plans that actually happen, without the
> back-and-forth*. Unlike *coordinating by hand in a group text*, our
> product *uses an AI assistant to juggle everyone's availability and
> preferences and propose a plan that works*.

All seven clauses are now either **evidenced by interview** or **refined
by research** — none are still resting on unconfirmed belief. Two clauses
changed shape after evidence came in, which is the point of the process:

- **Need:** softened from "the event gets abandoned" to "derailed,
  rescheduled, or dropped" — outright abandonment turned out to be the
  severe end of a spectrum, not the typical case.
- **Key benefit:** widened from a pure outcome ("plans that actually
  happen") to also name the hassle of coordinating as part of what's
  being relieved — an interview revealed the process itself, not just
  the result, mattered to the customer.
